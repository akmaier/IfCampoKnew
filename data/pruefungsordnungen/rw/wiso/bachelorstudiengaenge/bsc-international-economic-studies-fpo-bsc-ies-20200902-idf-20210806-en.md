## **Section 1 Scope** 

1These degree programme and examination regulations govern studies and examinations in the Bachelor's degree programme in International Business Studies at the School of Business, Economics and Society at the Faculty of Business, Economics, and Law at FAU, leading to the degree Bachelor of Science.[2] They supplement the currently valid version of the general examination regulations for Bachelor's degree programmes at the School of Business, Economics and Society at the Faculty of Business, Economics, and Law at FAU – **BPOWISO** – dated 1 August 2006. 

## **Section 2 Scope and Structure of the Degree Programme, Examinations, Teaching and Examination Language** 

(1) The Bachelor's degree programme in International Economic Studies at FAU is split into modules according to the **Appendix** . 

(2) The type and scope of the examinations are governed by Section 3 and the **Appendix** as well as Sections 17 to 20a of **BPOWISO** . 

(3) Section 3(5) **BPOWISO** applies with the proviso that the teaching and examination language is English and that individual teaching units and examinations in the (compulsory) elective modules may be held in German or other languages (in particular French and Spanish); otherwise, Section 3(5) **BPOWISO** shall remain unaffected.

## **Section 3 Interdisciplinary Specialisation Modules** 

(1)[1] The specialisation modules (electives) that can be chosen as part of the Bachelor's degree programme in International Economic Studies allow students to explore one or several specialist areas in more detail.[2] Students can select modules on the topics of business administration, economics, information systems, socioeconomics, and interdisciplinary subjects, or they can choose to complete a stay abroad. 

(2)[1] The first learning outcome of the specialisation modules in business administration is to allow students to explore in more detail the issue of decision-making in companies, with reference to planning, organisational aspects and accounting.[2] Secondly, the various choices available in the area of business administration offer students a wide range of options for specialisation.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to shape their own particular profile in view of their future career (or consecutive studies). 

(3)[1] The learning outcome of the specialisation modules from the area of economics is to allow students to gain a deeper insight into issues of economic policy at the level of the state using theoretical and empirical methods as well as analysing the optimal behaviour of individuals and companies in markets.[2] Secondly, the various choices available in the area of economics offer students a wide range of options for specialisation.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to shape their own particular profile in view of their future career (or consecutive studies). 

(4)[1] The learning outcome of the specialisation modules from information systems is to allow students to gain a deeper understanding of the integration of issues relevant to business operations from the fields of business administration and information systems.[2] Secondly, the various choices available in the area of information systems offer students a wide range of options for specialisation.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to shape their own particular profile in view of their future career (or consecutive studies). 

(5)[1] The learning outcome of the specialisation modules in socioeconomics is to allow students to gain deeper understanding of issues relating to the economy and society, as well as to use empirical methods to recognise socioeconomical problems and come up with structured solutions.[2] Secondly, the various choices available in the area of socioeconomics offer students a wide range of options for specialisation.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to shape their own particular profile in view of their future career (or consecutive studies). 

(6)[1] The learning outcome of the other interdisciplinary specialisation modules is firstly to allow students to explore interdisciplinary topics and areas in more detail and to train students to take an interdisciplinary approach.[2] Secondly, the various choices available in the interdisciplinary specialisation modules offer students a wide range of options for specialisation.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to shape their own particular profile in view of their future career (or consecutive studies). 

(7)[1] The first learning outcome of the stay abroad is to offer students the opportunity to explore interdisciplinary content and topics that are not part of the home university’s main research focus and which therefore cannot be studied in depth at the home university.[2] Secondly, the stay abroad allows students to gain international experience

and improve their intercultural skills.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice during the stay abroad offers students a wide range of specialisation options.[4] Fourthly, the wide range of options allows students to shape their own particular profile in view of their future career (or consecutive studies). 

(8)[1] The type and scope of the examination depend on the skills for the chosen module according to (2) to (6) and the module handbook.[2] The type and scope of the possible examinations are stipulated in Sections 17 to 20a **BPOWISO** .[3] The module catalog is announced before the beginning of the semester in accordance with local practice. 4Notwithstanding sentences 1 to 3, the type and scope of the examinations during the semester abroad depend on the regulations in place at the partner university, and learning agreements ought to be concluded before the stay abroad. 

(9)[1] Specialisation modules usually consist of a lecture (4 SWS), a seminar (4 SWS) or a lecture and a tutorial (each 2 SWS).[2] Further provisions shall be set forth in the module handbook.

(2)[1] The first amendment statute shall come into effect on 1 October 2022.
