## **Section 1 Scope** 

The degree programme and examination regulations for the research-oriented consecutive Master’s degree programme in Economics leading to the qualification Master of Science supplements the general examinations regulations for the Master’s degree programmes at the School of Business, Economics and Society at the Faculty of Business, Economics, and Law at FAU – **MPOWISO** – in the currently valid version.

## **Section 2 Qualification for a Master’s Degree Programme, Certificates and Admission Requirements** 

(1)[1] A subject-specific degree as defined by Section 26 (1)(1) **MPOWISO** is either a Bachelor’s degree in economics or the Bachelor’s degree in business studies with specialisation in economics at FAU pursuant to **FPO BA WiWi** .[2] The following shall be recognised as subject-related degrees within the meaning of Section 26 (1)(1) **MPO WISO** : 

1. all other Bachelor’s degrees in business studies and economics 

2. a Bachelor’s degree in mathematics 

3. a Bachelor’s degree in social science or behavioural science 

4. a Bachelor’s degree in a natural science 

5. a Bachelor’s degree in engineering 

6. a (Bachelor’s) degree in (business) law. 

(2)[1] Applicants shall be required to submit evidence of proficiency in English equivalent to at least level B2 of the Common European Framework of Reference as a further document within the meaning of 2.3.3 of the **Appendix** to **MPOWISO** .[ 2] The proof required according to sentence 1 may be provided, in particular, in the form of school certificates. These should demonstrate at least 5 years of language classes before obtaining the university entrance qualification; the final grade or the average grade in the last two years must be equivalent to at least the grade 4 (ausreichend – sufficient) or 5 points in the German grading system.[3] Proof of proficiency is not required if the applicant completed their first degree or university entrance qualification in English. 

(3)[1] Applicants with an outstanding first degree pursuant to (1)(1) shall be granted direct admission to the Master’s degree programme in Economics pursuant to 5.1 of the **Appendix MPOWISO** if they obtained a grade of 1.9 or better in the relevant degree programme.[2] Applicants with an overall grade in their degree pursuant to section 1 which is worse than 2.89 shall be deemed unsuitable and shall receive a rejection notification including reasons and information on legal remedies available.[3] Section 12 (4) sentences 1 to 3 **MPOWISO** shall apply accordingly for qualifications from a different grading system. 

(4) All applicants who are not granted direct admission to the Master’s degree programme pursuant to paragraph (3) who have completed a degree pursuant to paragraph (1) with the grade 2.89 or better must provide the following supporting documents as defined in 2.3.3 of the **Appendix** to **MPOWISO** , if available: 

1. Proof of time spent abroad (studying abroad, semester abroad, internships abroad, other work experience abroad) of at least three months (consecutively); a transcript of records or another certificate from the foreign university or a reference or similar document from the employer where the applicant completed work experience or an internship can be accepted as proof. 

2. Proof of relevant work experience or internships (application of economic concepts in practice or research); a contract of employment, a reference or a similar document from the employer can be accepted as proof.

## **Section 3 Scope and Structure of the Master’s Degree Programme, Examinations** 

(1)[1] In the first semester students study fundamental theories and methods in-depth (compulsory subjects = 30 ECTS credits).[2] In the second and third semester students choose a total of ten elective modules (5 ECTS credits each) in any combination they wish from five module groups (compulsory elective subjects); these must include at least one seminar in economics (5 ECTS credits).[ 3] In addition, students choose two modules (5 ECTS credits each) from all modules approved for this degree programme by the degree programme coordinators at the Faculty including the module groups mentioned above (free electives).[4] Modules not offered by the Faculty may be taken if approved beforehand.[5] Modules must be selected on the basis that the student will acquire more advanced knowledge and skills in economics in the Master’s degree programme in comparison to the Bachelor’s degree.[6] Students complete the Master’s thesis module in the fourth semester.[7] It consists of two examination achievements: the Master’s thesis (25 ECTS credits) and the Master’s seminar (5 ECTS credits). 

(2)[1] Students may choose between specialisations.[ 2] Students must complete a total of at least 15 ECTS credits in specialisation modules:[3] The following specialisations are offered: 

1. Labor economics 

2. Macroeconomics and finance 

3. Public economics 

4. Energy markets 

5. Health economics. 

4The module handbook shall specify which modules are allocated to which specialisations.[5] If a module may be allocated to more than one specialisation students may decide themselves which specialisation it is to be allocated to.[6] Upon request, students may receive written confirmation of up to three specialisations that they have studied. 

(3) Type and scope of the examinations are governed by the **Appendix** and Sections 16 – 18b **MPOWISO** . 

(4)[1] Section 4 (5) **MPOWISO** shall apply with the proviso that the teaching and examination language in the compulsory modules is English.[2] Compulsory elective modules and elective modules may also be offered and chosen in which the teaching and examination language is German. 

## **Section 4 Compulsory Elective Subjects** 

(1)[1] The learning outcome of the compulsory elective module groups ‘Labour economics’, ‘Macroeconomics and finance’, ‘Public economics’, ‘Energy markets’ and ‘Health economics’ is to give students the opportunity to explore the theory of one or more of the specialist field(s) stated in section 3 (2).[2] The second learning outcome is methodological, training students in specific approaches and applying methodological skills acquired through the compulsory subjects to various fields of modern economics. 3Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to create an individual portfolio of modules with a focus on methodology or different topics, tailoring their own profile to suit their future career.[4] Section 3 (1)(2) shall be complied with when selecting individual modules. 

5 

(2)[1] The type and scope of the examination are dependent on the skills for the chosen module according to paragraph (1) and the module handbook.[2] Examination achievements for compulsory elective subjects can be either: written examination, oral examination, seminar paper, written assignment, short test, discussion contribution, presentation/presentation paper, handout, project work, internship report or a combination of the above; section 6 (2)(3) **MPOWISO** shall remain unaffected.[3] The module catalogue is announced before the beginning of the semester in accordance with local practice. 

(3)[1] As a rule, elective compulsory modules either comprise a combination of a lecture (2 semester hours) and a tutorial (1-2 semester hours), or a seminar (3 semester hours).[2] Further provisions shall be set forth in the module handbook. 

## **Section 5 Legal Validity and Transitory Provisions** 

(1)[1] These degree programme and examination regulations shall come into effect on the day after their publication.[2] They shall apply to all students who enter the Master’s degree programme in Economics in the winter semester 2009/2010 or later. 

(2)[1] The seventh amendment statute shall come into effect on 1 October 2017.[2] It shall apply to all students starting the degree programme from the winter semester 2017/2018 onwards. 

(3)[1] The eighth amendment statute shall come into effect on the day after its publication.[2] Notwithstanding sentence 1, the amendments in section 2 shall apply to all students starting a degree programme from the winter semester 2020/2021 onwards.
