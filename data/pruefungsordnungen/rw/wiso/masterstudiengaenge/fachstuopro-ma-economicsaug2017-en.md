## **Section 1 Scope** 

The degree programme and examination regulations for the research-oriented consecutive Master's degree programme in Economics leading to the qualification Master of Science supplements the general examinations regulations for the consecutive and nonconsecutive Master's degree programmes at the School of Business and Economics at the Faculty of Business, Economics, and Law at FAU – **MPOWIWI** – in the currently valid version. 

## **Section 2 Qualification for a Master's Degree Programme, Certificates and Admission Requirements** 

(1)[1] A subject-specific degree within the meaning of Section 26 (1)(1) **MPOWIWI** is a Bachelor's degree in economics.[2] The following shall be recognised as subject-related degrees within the meaning of Section 26 (1)(1) **MPOWIWI** : 

1. a Bachelor's degree in mathematics 

2. a Bachelor's degree in social science or behavioural science 

3. a Bachelor's degree in a natural science 

4. a Bachelor's degree in engineering 

5. a (Bachelor's) degree in (business) law. 

(2)[1] The following documents within the meaning of Appendix No. 2.3.3 **MPOWIWI** must be submitted: 

1. proof of any stays abroad (if applicable) 

2. proof of any relevant interships (if applicable) 

3. proof of sufficient English language proficiency equivalent to at least level B2 according to the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages 

2The proof required according to sentence 1 (3) may be provided, in particular, in the form of school certificates. These should demonstrate at least 5 years of language classes before obtaining the university entrance qualification; the final grade or the average grade in the last two years must be equivalent to at least the grade of 4 (ausreichend – sufficient) or 5 points respectively in the German grading system. 

(3) In the first stage of the qualification assessment process, the documents to be submitted according to Appendix No. 2.3 **MPOWIWI** and paragraph 2 above are assessed according to the following criteria, with the maximum number of available points given in parentheses according to Appendix No. 5.1 **MPOWIWI** : 

1. quality of the previous degree or previous achievements (maximum of 50 points) 

2. subject knowledge, skills and abilities (maximum 50 points) 

(4)[1] In the second stage of the qualification assessment process according to Appendix No. 5.2 **MPOWIWI** , applicants are assessed on the basis of the documents submitted showing additional qualifications such as relevant internships, English language proficiency, stays abroad (maximum of 10 points in total) and a qualification assessment interview (maximum of 10 points).[2] The interview shall have a duration of approximately 20 minutes and is intended to demonstrate that the applicant possesses the required technical and methodological expertise and can be expected to carry out independent academic work in a more research-orientated degree programme.[3] The qualification assessment interview shall cover the qualification criteria given in (3) (2). 

**Section 3 Scope and Structure of the Master's Degree Programme, Examinations** 

(1)[1] In the first semester students study fundamental theories and methods in-depth (compulsory subjects = 30 ECTS credits).[2] In the second and third semester students choose a total of ten elective modules (5 ECTS credits each) in any combination they wish from five module groups (compulsory elective subjects); these must include at least one seminar in economics (5 ECTS credits).[ 3] In addition, students choose two modules (5 ECTS credits each) from all modules approved for this degree programme by the degree programme coordinators at the Faculty including the module groups mentioned above (free electives).[4] Modules not offered by the Faculty may be taken if approved beforehand.[5] When choosing modules, a subject-specific increase in expertise in the Master's degree programme in economics compared to the preceding Bachelor's degree must be proven.[6] Students complete the Master's thesis module in the fourth semester.[7] It consists of two examination achievements: the Master's thesis (25 ECTS credits) and the Master's seminar (5 ECTS credits). 

(2)[1] Students may choose to study specialisations.[ 2] Specialisations are connected fields of study in which a minimum of 15 ECTS points are to be awarded.[3] The following fields of study are available as specialisations: 

1. Labor economics 

2. Macroeconomics and finance 

3. Public economics 

4. Energy markets 

5. Health economics. 

4The module handbook shall specify which modules are allocated to which specialisations.[5] If a module may be allocated to more than one specialisation students may decide themselves which specialisation it is to be allocated to.[6] Upon request, students may receive written confirmation of up to three specialisations that they have studied. 

(3) Type and scope of the examinations are governed by the **Appendix** and Sections 16 – 18 **MPOWIWI** . 

(4)[1] Section 4 (5) **MPOWIWI** shall apply with the proviso that the teaching and examination language in the compulsory modules is English.[2] Compulsory elective modules and elective modules may also be offered and chosen in which the teaching and examination language is German. 

## **Section 4 Compulsory Elective Subjects** 

(1)[1] The learning outcome of the compulsory elective module groups ‘Labour economics’, ‘Macroeconomics and finance’, ‘Public economics’, ‘Energy markets’ and ‘Health economics’ is to give students the opportunity to explore the theory of one or more of the specialist field(s) stated in Section 3 (2).[2] The second learning outcome is methodological, training students in specific approaches and applying methodological skills acquired through the compulsory subjects to various fields of modern economics.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice gives students the opportunity to create an individual portfolio of modules with a focus on methodology or different topics, tailoring their own profile to suit their future career.[4] Section 3 (1) (2) shall be complied with when selecting individual modules. 

(2)[1] The type and scope of the examination are dependent on the competencies for the chosen module according to (1) and the module handbook.[2] Examination achievements for compulsory elective subjects can be either: written examination, oral examination, seminar paper, written assignment, discussion contribution, presentation/presentation paper, handout, research project, internship report or a combination of the above.[3] The module catalogue is announced before the beginning of the seminar in accordance with local practice. 

(3)[1] As a rule, elective compulsory modules either comprise a combination of a lecture (2 semester hours) and a tutorial (1-2 semester hours), or a seminar (3 semester hours). 2Further provisions shall be set forth in the module handbook. 

## **Section 5 Legal Validity and Transitory Provisions** 

(1)[1] These degree programme and examination regulations shall come into effect on the day after their publication.[2] They shall apply to all students who enter the Master's degree programme in Economics in the winter semester 2009/2010 or later. 

(2)[1] The seventh amendment statute shall come into effect on 1 October 2017.[2] It shall apply to all students starting a degree programme from the winter semester 2017/2018 onwards.
