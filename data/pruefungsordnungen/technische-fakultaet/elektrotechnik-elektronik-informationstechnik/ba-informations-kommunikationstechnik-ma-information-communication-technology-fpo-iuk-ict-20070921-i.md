## **Part 1: General provisions** 

## **Section 35 Scope** 

The degree program and examination regulations for the consecutive Bachelor’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology (IuK) and the Master’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology (ICT) complement the currently valid General Examination Regulations for Bachelor’s and Master’s degree programs at the Faculty of Engineering at FAU – **ABMPO/TechFak** . 

## **Section 36 Bachelor’s Degree Program, Related Degree Programs** 

(1) The Bachelor’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology is made up of modules worth a total of 180 ECTS credits pursuant to **Appendix 1** . 

(2)[1] The following Bachelor’s degree programs are considered subject-related degree programs within the meaning of Section 24 (1)(2)(2) **ABMPO/TechFak** :[2] In justified exceptional circumstances, the Degree Program Committee may allow exceptions from sentence 1. 

## **Section 37 Master’s Degree Program, Standard Duration of Study, Part-time Study, Start of Degree Program, Teaching and Examination Language, Related Degree Programs** 

(1)[1] The Master’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology builds on the contents of the Bachelor’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology.[2] It shall consist of modules worth 120 ECTS credits in total pursuant to **Appendix 2** or **3** and shall include a Master’s thesis with a six-month period for thesis work, including a presentation. 

2 

(2)[1] The Master’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology can be completed as a full-time or as a part-time degree program.[2] The Master's degree program in Information and Communication Technology can be started in either the summer or the winter semester. 

(3)[1] Notwithstanding Section 4 (5)(1) **ABMPO/TechFak** , the teaching and examination language in the Master’s degree program in Information and Communication Technology is English.[2] Individual courses and examinations may be conducted in German. 3The Master’s thesis shall be written in English; the Examinations Committee shall decide on any exceptions at the student’s request.[4] The degree certificate and final academic record shall be issued in German and English. 

(4)[1] The following Bachelor’s degree programs are considered subject-related degree programs within the meaning of Section 30 (3)(2) **ABMPO/TechFak** :[2] In justified exceptional circumstances, the Degree Program Committee may allow exceptions from sentence 1. 

## **Part II: Special Provisions** 

## **1. Bachelor’s Examination** 

**Section 38 Structure of the Bachelor’s Degree Program** 

(1)[1] The Bachelor’s degree program consists of compulsory, compulsory elective and elective modules.[2] The distribution across the semesters, the type and duration of the examinations and the required number of ECTS credits are stipulated in **Appendix 1** . 

(2) Modules no. 1 to 22 in **Appendix 1** are compulsory. 

(3)[1] Modules nos. 23, 26 and 27 in **Appendix 1** (compulsory elective modules, seminar and laboratory course ) are modules which must be chosen from catalogs drawn up by the IuK Degree Program Committee and published on the IuK website.[2] Upon request, the chairperson of the Degree Program Committee can allow other modules.[3] Further provisions are stipulated in Sections 38a and 38b. 

(4)[1] Module no. 24 in **Appendix 1** is a module worth 5 ECTS credits which must be chosen from the modules offered by the Department of Electrical, Electronic and Communication Engineering or the Department of Computer Science.[2] Students may also choose two modules worth 2.5 ECTS credits each. ## **Section 38a Compulsory Elective Modules** 

(1)[1] The learning outcome for the compulsory elective modules from the module catalog for the degree program in Information and Communication Technology (no. 23 **Appendix 1** ) worth a total of 10 ECTS credits is to allow students to specialize in certain selected skills such as information and communication system design and programming.[2] Students gain analytical skills and can formulate mathematical descriptions for technical contexts.[3] The second learning outcome has a research focus, with students learning subject-related methods of research and exploring their subject in more depth. 4Thirdly, the element of choice allows students to tailor their profile in view of their career plans. 

(2)[1] The type and scope of the examination are dependent on the skills for the relevant module according to paragraph (1) and the module handbook.[2] Possible examination achievements per module are: Written examination (90, 90 or 120 mins) or oral examination (30 mins).[3] The module handbook is published before the beginning of the semester in accordance with local practice. 

(3)[1] Compulsory elective modules amounting to 5 ECTS credits usually consist of a lecture (2 SWS) and a tutorial (2 SWS) or a lecture (3 SWS) and a tutorial (1 SWS). 2Any exceptions are detailed in the module handbook. 

## **Section 38b Seminar Module and Laboratory Course Module** 

(1)[1] The learning outcome of the seminar module and the lab course module (nos. 26 and 27 in **Appendix 1** ) is to allow students to gather, analyze and interpret information relevant to the design of information and communication systems and programming based on scientific principles.[2] A second learning outcome is aimed at promoting personal and social skills through preparing, reporting on and presenting a topic relating to the subject for a specialist audience at a Bachelor’s level and in a manner tailored to suit the target group, as well as working under supervision in a group to develop and test subject-related applications and possibilities for implementation with respect to the chosen subject.[3] Thirdly, the element of choice allows students to tailor their profile in view of their career plans. 

(2)[1] The available modules are listed in the relevant catalog (see Section 38(3)).[2] Further information about the type and scope of lectures and seminars and the examination is available in **Appendix 1** and the module handbook.

## **Section 42 Bachelor’s Thesis** 

(1)[1] The Bachelor’s thesis enables students to learn to solve problems independently in the field of information and communications technology.[2] All full-time university lecturers employed at the Department of Electrical, Electronic and Communication Engineering and at the Department of Computer Science shall be entitled to assign Bachelor’s theses.[3] Requirements for the Bachelor’s thesis shall be such that it can be completed with a workload of 300 hours.

## **Section 45 Scope and Structure of the Master’s Degree Program** 

(1)[1] The Master’s degree program consists of compulsory, compulsory elective and elective modules.[2] Students are also required to complete a research project worth 5 ECTS credits at an ICT Chair or a company which has a cooperation agreement with an ICT Chair.[3] Instead of a research project within the meaning of sentence 2, the chairperson of the Degree Program Committee may also approve other ungraded elective modules from the course catalog of the Faculty of Engineering worth 5 ECTS credits.[4] The distribution of the modules pursuant to sentences 1 and 3 across the semesters, the type and duration of the examinations and the required number of ECTS credits are set forth in **Appendix 2** and **3** . 

(2)[1] Each student chooses their own specialization in order to establish a subject-specific profile. The specializations available are 

- Embedded Systems, 

- Networks and Digital Communication, 

- Media Processing Systems. 

Section 45a stipulates more details concerning the learning outcomes of the individual specializations.

**Section 45a Learning outcomes of specializations** 

(1)[1] The overriding learning outcome of the specializations which can be chosen pursuant to Section 45 (2) (module group 1) is to allow students to gain a more advanced knowledge of selected specialist areas.[2] They acquire a wide range of methodologies and up-to-date knowledge of engineering in the field of information and communication technology and can apply them to solve problems in this area.[3] This should allow students to acquire skills of relevance to research. 

(2)[1] In the specialization ‘Embedded systems’, students acquire skills in the areas of application designing embedded systems and associated design methodology.[2] The students gain knowledge ranging from languages for specifying, modeling and synthesizing mixed hardware and software systems to testing implementation in the field.[2] This specialization is based on the acquisition of methodologies from information technology, signal and image processing and on integrated circuit design. 

(3)[1] In the specialization ‘Networks and Digital Communications’, students acquire skills in the areas of application of wired and wireless digital data transmission as well as architectures and communication network protocols located on higher layers.[2] It covers a broad range of content related to analogue and digital electronics, from information, signal and coding theory to IT concepts for the operation of large-scale communications networks serving numerous fixed-line and mobile devices. 

(4)[1] In the specialization ‘Media Processing Systems’, students acquire skills in the area of application of designing media processing systems.[2] It covers methods for recording, transferring and saving multimedia data as well as skills in analyzing, interpreting and converting such data.[3] Students become confident in the application of procedures for source compression, pattern recognition and signal analysis.

## **Section 45d Research Project** 

1The learning outcome of the research project module is to allow students to learn how to put academic work to practical use in research.[2] Upon completion of the research laboratory course, students are able to independently conduct literature research and classify and analyze literature sources.[3] Furthermore, students are able to independently discuss the task set for them from the field of information and communication technology within a given period of time using scientific methods and to develop suitable approaches and concepts for solving it.[4] In addition, they have the knowledge required to present their scientific research results in presentations and research reports. 5The research work can be either experimental, theoretical or constructive in nature. 6Several areas of specialization can be combined.
